The preparation for a photo is far more valuable than the moment of capture. This is the biggest lesson I've learned after working three and a half years in the Communications Department for a prominent construction Union in Ontario. This goes beyond gear preparation and technical planning for the shoot. It's about earning the right to be in the position to get the cleanest shot possible. This is an invisible barrier many aspiring professionals never manage to cross. 

The invisible barrier is your positioning. Savvy event producers will reserve the clearest viewing angles for the media for a reason. They know if they want to be presented well, they need to prioritize the people covering the event. For this reason, gaining legitimate access to these physical locations means you need to come from an established and respected organization with a proven body of work.

This is where a lot of people go wrong. Having the world's best camera will noticeably raise the floor of your picture quality, but you'll never be in the spot to capture the iconic front page shot.

---
# Building a Body of Work
My firsthand experience of this was covering Mark Carney's Liberal Campaign Tour in 2025. Looking through the viewfinder and pressing the shutter was the easiest part of the shoot. The hardest part was earning my spot in the room. 

It started with building a body of work. I took two years to build a portfolio with the purpose of increasing my employability. It's one thing to tell people you are a photographer and have the latest mirrorless hanging off your neck. It's a different discussion when you showcase a curation of projects. Over two and a half years I built my body of work between an educational tech startup, fitness equipment manufacturer, and personal livestream. Developing this portfolio not only got employers and recruiters taking me seriously, it landed me my first Communications role. 

Upon signing on, I realized this Construction Union had municipal and provincial politicians visiting the head office frequently. After covering a few events I made a mental promise to myself:

>"Should a legitimate VIP visit the office, I will be responsible for photographing the big moment."

For two and a half years, I made it a personal mission to ensure any photo assignment I was responsible for would be shot prioritizing quality and speed. Anything that got published was taken in its highest fidelity and subject to the post-production process. While there is value in speed and being "first to post", my role was different in the sense that the body of work I created had more value in its archival of Union history. People would be pulling from this archive for historical reference in the coming decades.

Doug Ford's 2025 campaign tour served as the warm-up assignment. The Progressive Conservative Party of Ontario made the Oakville Head Office one of their campaign stops to gain the support of the heavy machinery operators. This is where the workflow was put to the test. While it required additional work on my end, the results were clear. By shooting as much as possible, cutting hundreds of shots, editing the best stills, and handing off deliverables within the hour, I showcased that I was able to produce timeless images under pressure.

This was one of many examples that helped foster a positive relationship with my manager and Executive Leadership. My manager knew he could count on me and executive leadership was proud knowing the organization was [presented with prestige](https://iuoelocal793.org/local-793-endorses-pc-party-of-ontario-in-2024-election/). If an upcoming marquee assignment was to be handed off to the Communications team in the future, I knew I would be entrusted to capture the big moment. 

That marquee assignment came midweek through a memo: Liberal Party Leader Mark Carney would be making a campaign stop at IUOE Local 793 Head Office and I was deemed the Communications Department's photography lead.

After four and a half years of building a body of work and earning institutional trust, I was front row of the press junket as Mark Carney announced, should he be elected, he would be implementing a new federal grant for students who were entering the trades. Pressing the shutter was the easiest part of my day. The hardest part was earning my spot in the room.

# In Closing
Taking this beyond communications, your access to these events is everything. Prioritizing your access requires a cohesive body of work, institutional trust, and established reliability. High-quality consistency isn't something that is established over a few days or weeks. It's built over years of deliberate focus. 

Once you've established your access, there's a sense of irony in accepting that you don't know what is going to happen that day. There could be a historic announcement for the country. A heckler may ruin the address. Whatever occurs, your job is to document it and inform your audience of what took place.
